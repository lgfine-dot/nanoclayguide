Ask a supplier whether nanoclay is safe to handle and you'll get a reassuring answer, and the reassurance is mostly earned. Ask an industrial hygienist the same question and you'll get a more careful one, because the hygienist is thinking about a different substance than the supplier is.

That gap is the subject of this article. The clay mineral is not the hazard people should be most worried about. The thing that travels with it usually is.

## Start with what the clay itself does

Montmorillonite, kaolinite, and halloysite are not exotic materials that arrived with the nanotechnology era. Humans have been swallowing bentonite deliberately for a very long time — as an antacid, as an anti-diarrheal, as a clarifying agent in wine and juice processing, and in some cultures simply as food. This history means the oral toxicology dataset is unusually deep for a material that gets marketed as a nanomaterial, and it is consistently unremarkable. Oral acute toxicity in rodent models sits well above any exposure a worker could plausibly get from handling powder in a plant.

Dermal exposure is similarly quiet for the natural clays. Bentonite and kaolin appear across the cosmetics industry in leave-on and rinse-off products at meaningful concentrations without a signal.

Organoclays are the exception worth flagging. The quaternary ammonium surfactants used to make clay organophilic are mild skin and eye irritants in their own right. Handling organoclay powder is not the same hazard as handling untreated bentonite, and the difference is entirely the modifier. Gloves and eye protection are not optional here.

## The inhalation question is the real one

Everything above concerns routes that mostly do not matter in a plant. The route that matters is inhalation, and the picture changes.

Clay platelets are not, in themselves, classified carcinogens. The problem is that bentonite ore comes out of the ground with quartz in it, and respirable crystalline silica is a well-characterized cause of silicosis, with epidemiological evidence also linking it to lung cancer, tuberculosis, and airways disease.

This matters because the exposure limits are dramatically different depending on which substance you're measuring:

**Particulates Not Otherwise Regulated (PNOR).** Where clay dust contains negligible quartz, it falls under the generic nuisance-dust limits. In the US, OSHA sets these at 15 mg/m³ for total particulate and 5 mg/m³ for the respirable fraction, both as eight-hour time-weighted averages.

**Respirable crystalline silica.** If quartz is present at more than trace levels, a completely different limit applies. OSHA's 2016 respirable crystalline silica standard set a permissible exposure limit of 0.05 mg/m³ as an eight-hour TWA, aligning with the NIOSH recommended exposure limit that had stood since 1974. ACGIH's threshold limit value for α-quartz is lower still, at 0.025 mg/m³.

Look at the arithmetic. The respirable silica limit is **one hundred times more stringent** than the respirable nuisance-dust limit. An operation that is comfortably compliant under one framework can be badly out of compliance under the other, and the only thing separating the two is the quartz content of the material you bought.

## The number to ask your supplier for

This leads to the single most useful action in this whole article: **get the free crystalline silica content of your nanoclay in writing.**

Purified, commercial nanoclay grades are generally processed to bring free silica below 1%, which is the threshold below which most nuisance-dust frameworks apply. Crude, unpurified, or low-grade material can be substantially higher. If a supplier cannot produce a certificate of analysis or a test report with a silica number on it, that absence is itself the answer, and it should change how you handle the material.

A supplier's safety data sheet is the right place to look first, but treat older SDS documents with caution: many were written before nano-specific hazard communication expectations tightened, and they may not characterize the product accurately as a nanomaterial even when it plainly is one.

## Does "nano" change the inhalation picture?

This is the question people expect a nanoclay article to dodge, so let's take it directly.

The theoretical concern is that nanoscale particles behave differently in the lung than micron-scale ones: they deposit more deeply, they present enormous surface area, and for some engineered nanomaterials this translates into inflammatory responses that bulk material doesn't produce.

The practical counterweight for clays is that a fully exfoliated single platelet is largely a laboratory condition, not a warehouse one. Dry nanoclay powder is a mass of aggregates and agglomerates. What becomes airborne when you cut open a bag is generally in the respirable-dust size range, not the single-platelet range. This is a meaningful distinction and it is also the basis on which several regulators have declined to treat clay powders as presenting a categorically novel inhalation hazard.

That said, it would be overclaiming to say the question is settled. Nano-specific toxicology for layered silicates is a smaller literature than the bulk-clay literature, and it is still growing. The responsible position is: treat nanoclay dust as fine mineral dust, control it accordingly, and do not assume the nanoscale fraction is irrelevant simply because it is inconvenient to measure.

## What competent handling actually looks like

None of this requires exotic protocols. It requires the fine-powder discipline that a well-run plant already has.

**Engineering controls first.** Local exhaust ventilation at transfer points, enclosed handling for bag dumping and weighing, and dust collection on any operation that generates airborne powder. This is where the exposure reduction actually happens, and PPE is a backstop, not a substitute.

**Respiratory protection matched to the material.** Where engineering controls cannot hold exposures below the applicable limit, respiratory protection is required — and the applicable limit depends on the silica content, which is why the certificate of analysis is not paperwork but a control decision.

**Skin and eye protection, especially for organoclays.** Gloves and eye protection for all powder handling; treat the surfactant as the irritant it is.

**Exposure monitoring, documented.** Air sampling for the respirable fraction, with silica speciation where the material or the ore source warrants it. Keep the records — they matter for compliance, for customer audits, and for occupational health surveillance.

**Medical surveillance where silica exposure is credible.** If your operation handles crude clay, mines it, or mills it, periodic medical examinations for exposed workers are the standard of care, not an abundance of caution.

**Housekeeping without dry sweeping.** Dry sweeping re-aerosolizes settled dust and undoes the controls. Wet methods or HEPA vacuuming.

## The bottom line

Nanoclay has one of the more reassuring safety profiles among nanomaterials, and the reassurance is not marketing. The mineral has a long human-exposure record and the toxicology is genuinely favorable by the oral and dermal routes.

But "the clay is safe" and "the dust is safe" are different claims, and only the first one is well supported. The occupational hazard in nanoclay handling is fine mineral dust generally and crystalline silica specifically, and the difference between a compliant operation and a non-compliant one can come down to a single number on a certificate of analysis that most buyers never ask for.

Ask for it.
